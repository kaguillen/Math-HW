\begin{tcolorbox}
    \begin{problem}{3.19} Let $G$ and $H$ be groups. Does there exist a product $G$ and $H$ in \textbf{Grp}?        
    \end{problem}
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{proof}
    Yes. The product will be the direct product of the groups $G$ and $H$, and we know this forms a new group $G\times H$ that will be an object in \textbf{Grp}, together with,
    \begin{align*}
        p: G\times H &\to G \\
        (g,h) &\mapsto g \\
        q: G\times H & \to H \\
        (g,h) &\mapsto h.
    \end{align*}
    We see for any group in $Z \in $ \textbf{Grp}, we have the following bijective map,
    \begin{align*}
        \phi: \text{Hom}_\textbf{Grp}(Z, G\times H) &\to \text{Hom}_\textbf{Grp}(Z,G) \times \text{Hom}_\textbf{Grp}(Z,H) \\
        f &\mapsto (p \circ f, q \circ f).
    \end{align*}
    We will prove that it is bijective by first proving it is injective.

    Consider any $f,f' \in \text{Hom}_\textbf{Grp}(Z, G\times H)$. If $\phi(f) = \phi(f')$ we see the following,
    \begin{align*}
        \phi(f) &= \phi(f') \\
        (p \circ f, q \circ f) &= (p \circ f', q \circ f')\\
         \rightarrow (p\circ f) = (p\circ f') &\text{ and } (q \circ f) = (q \circ f') 
    \end{align*}

    but $p$ and $q$ are injective, and thus $f = f'$.
\end{proof}
